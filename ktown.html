LA Kings
K-Town Night

The first NHL program built around Korean-American culture, created from a data case, carried by a harmonica ensemble of Koreatown seniors, and watched by ESPN and Seoul.

RoleCreator & Organizer, LA Kings
PartnerKoreatown Senior & Community Center (KSCC)
RunSeason 1 (2025) · Season 2 (Jan 2026), now annual
Recognition▸ ESPN · The Korea Daily · coverage in South Korea

01The idea: K-Town, not Korea

When I joined the LA Kings in late 2023, I was the only Korean employee in the organization. The team had hosted nights celebrating Filipino, Armenian, and Mexican communities, but nothing for the Korean American community next door, even though Los Angeles is home to one of the largest Korean populations anywhere in the world.

I'm a business-intelligence analyst, so I made the case the way I make every case: with data. I pulled ticketing patterns, audience geography, and the steady climb of interest in Korean culture, and proposed a deliberate choice, call it "K-Town Night," not "Korea Night." A country is an abstraction. Koreatown is a place with a main street, a community center, and grandparents in it. That specificity is what turns a theme night into a genuine point of connection.

02The harmonica that became a symbol

For the performance, we could have booked a K-pop dance team. Instead we partnered with the Koreatown Senior & Community Center and its harmonica ensemble, about thirteen seniors, all Korean immigrants or Korean Americans, who had practiced weekly for over a year. Many of them learned the harmonica as children in Korea and hadn't played since.

In hanbok, led by 80-year-old Donna Lee, they played the US national anthem before a playoff game against Edmonton. It went viral instantly. They came back for Game 2 in Kings jerseys, and again for Game 5.

"We were able to stand shoulder-to-shoulder as a Korean American, and not just as an immigrant." Kwan-Il Park · Executive Director, KSCC
"I could have never imagined that this would happen. We're all L.A. Kings fans now!" Donna Lee · 80, lead performer

03What it took

None of this was in my job description. My actual role is analyzing large-scale data, ticket-purchase histories, website usage, and marketing performance, and integrating machine learning and LLMs to find opportunities for executives. Coordinating performers, shaping a run-of-show, and producing an on-ice cultural moment was well outside that lane.

But the project needed someone who understood both the language and the culture to see it through, and I believed it was worth the extra load if it was good for the team and the community. It turned out to be both.

04The result, and season two

The harmonica group became the unofficial symbol of K-Town Night. The story reached ESPN and traveled all the way back to South Korea, riding the same wave of attention as K-pop and K-dramas. Internally, it became a proof point for the organization's move toward community-centered strategy - the idea that data and culture, combined, can create experiences fans genuinely connect with.

Season two, in January 2026, was bigger in every way: the harmonica group returned to a dedicated stage built on the ice, and we released commemorative K-Town Night apparel made in collaboration with Korean American rapper Dumbfoundead. What started as one analyst's proposal is now an annual fixture on the NHL calendar.
